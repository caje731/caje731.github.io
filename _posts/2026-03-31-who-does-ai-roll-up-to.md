The question now turns up across the industry with increasing regularity: where should the AI buck stop? Who does it roll up to?

It tends to arise when AI stops being a novelty and starts becoming a budget line. The pilots are over. The spending is real. The risks are clearer, and everyone agrees the subject matters. That is when the argument begins.

At first glance, it looks like an org chart question.

If AI is a technology capability, the CTO should own it. If it is a once-a-generation company-wide transformation, that points to the CEO. If the point is productivity, cost, and capital allocation, the CFO has a case. If work itself is being redesigned, the COO is already involved. And if the company wants a dedicated executive to drive the agenda, it creates a Chief AI Officer.

Every answer sounds plausible. None is complete.

That's because the question, while legit, is a red herring. The real question is not who owns AI, but who owns which decisions about AI.
The deeper question is really about how a company coordinates a capability that cuts across technology, finance, operations, and strategy without diffusing accountability.

## Why this gets so slippery

Most executive debates are clearer than this. Cybersecurity has a center of gravity, and finance systems do too. AI does not.

It is a platform, a tool, a cost line, a governance problem, and a redesign of work all at once. That is why the debate rarely settles for long: companies are trying to force a cross-cutting issue into a single box on the org chart.

## The CEO case

There is a serious case for the CEO.

The biggest AI decisions are not about models so much as ambition, speed, risk appetite, where to place bets, and which parts of the business get reinvented rather than merely optimized. Those are CEO calls.

If AI gets treated as another IT programme, the company will likely produce a few useful tools and miss the bigger point: a better support bot, a faster report, a slicker workflow. Useful, certainly, but not transformative.

Only the CEO can force the hard trade-offs:

- Where do we automate, and where do we differentiate?
- Which capabilities should be shared, and which should stay local?
- How much risk are we willing to take for speed?
- Which legacy processes are we finally prepared to kill?

The limitation is obvious: CEOs can create urgency, but they do not usually run platforms or redesign workflows themselves.

## The CTO case

Early on, the CTO may have the strongest claim.

Before strategy comes capability. Can teams access models securely? Is the data usable? Are there guardrails on cost, latency, quality, and security? Can anyone get beyond a demo? Those are technology questions.

Without technical stewardship, AI quickly becomes disorderly: duplicated vendors, inconsistent controls, scattered pilots, and no reliable path to production.

That is why the CTO often ends up building the foundation: shared tooling, standards, governance, and a route from experiment to scale.

But there is a trap here. Put AI entirely in the technology bucket and the company starts obsessing over models, architecture, and platforms while ignoring workflow redesign, incentives, and adoption. Plenty gets built, but too little changes.

## The Chief AI Officer case

This is the newest answer: create a Chief AI Officer.

The logic is easy to follow. AI cuts across functions, the existing C-suite is busy, and a dedicated executive can in theory drive focus, coordinate efforts, and keep the agenda moving. Sometimes that works.

A strong Chief AI Officer can stop AI from dissolving into side projects and committee talk by connecting strategy to execution and keeping pressure on the system.

But the role can also become a convenient fiction. The rest of the leadership team quietly outsources the issue, while the new executive inherits responsibility without full control. The technology still depends on the CTO, the money still depends on the CFO, operating changes still depend on business leaders, and ambition still depends on the CEO.

So the title does not remove the ambiguity. At best, it can make it more manageable.

## The CFO case

The CFO's claim is less fashionable, but it is substantial.

AI is now a capital allocation issue. The spending is significant, the vendor market is noisy, and the ROI claims are often optimistic.

Someone has to ask the hard questions:

- Which use cases actually move revenue, margin, or cycle time?
- What are the unit economics at scale?
- Are we funding a real capability or just a portfolio of demos?
- What labour assumptions are hiding inside the business case?

That discipline matters.

The danger is overcorrection. If every AI investment has to survive a narrow near-term ROI test, the company may discard capabilities that only pay back once they are adopted broadly.

## The COO case, and everyone else's

If AI changes how work gets done, the COO is already in the story.

The biggest gains often do not come from adding intelligence to one step. They come from removing steps, collapsing handoffs, and redesigning decisions. That is operating model work.

The CHRO has a stake too, because roles and skills will shift. Legal and risk matter because governance bolted on late is governance that fails. Product and commercial leaders matter because customer-facing AI changes experience, pricing, and differentiation.

This, to my mind, is the central point: AI is not just a technical stack. It is a management problem expressed through technology.

## So who should own it?

The practical answer is that no one person can sensibly own all of it.

The CEO should own the ambition and the enterprise trade-offs. The CTO should own the technical foundation. The CFO should own investment discipline. Business and operations leaders should own adoption and redesign. A Chief AI Officer, where one exists, should act as an integrator rather than a final repository for the problem.

That may sound untidy, and it is. But it is still more honest than pretending one executive can carry a problem this cross-cutting.

The better question is not who owns AI, but who owns which decisions.

Companies have a habit of creating councils, task forces, and new titles in the hope that ambiguity will disappear. Sometimes they help. Just as often, they simply give the organisation somewhere to send the problem.

I am wary of neat answers here. AI does not fail because nobody cares; it fails because everyone cares about a different slice of it, and nobody resolves the seams.

If one wants a shorthand, it is this: AI should roll up to the CEO for accountability, the CTO for capability, the CFO for discipline, and the business for results, with a Chief AI Officer sometimes acting as connective tissue.

That ambiguity is not a flaw in the model. It is the work.
